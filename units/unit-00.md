# Unit 0 — Choosing Your Spine Project

**No reading assigned.**
**Load:** Light — one focused sitting.

Work through the three phases below before starting Unit 1. The goal is to find and commit to the project you'll carry through the entire course.

---

## Phase 1: Problem inventory

*Generate candidates before filtering them. Aim for 5–8. Don't evaluate yet — breadth first.*

Work through these prompts:

- What do you keep wishing someone would fix?
- What do you (or people around you) work around regularly — a process, a tool, a situation?
- What takes longer than it should, or produces inconsistent results despite consistent effort?
- Where do things get lost, confused, or duplicated?
- What problem keeps coming back even after you've "solved" it once?
- What would you fix if you had a free month and no other obligations?
- Where do you feel the gap between how things are and how they should be most acutely?

## Phase 2: Course-fitness filter

*Now evaluate your candidates. A good spine project needs all five of the following.*

**1. Real stakeholder** — Is there a genuine need with someone (even just you) who actually cares about the outcome? A workflow you rely on counts. A purely hypothetical system you might build someday doesn't.

**2. Non-obvious problem** — If you already know the right solution, pick something else. The course is about diagnosing what's actually wrong before designing a fix. If you can describe the solution in a sentence before doing any diagnosis, the project will teach you very little.

**3. Enough complexity** — Does it have multiple interacting parts, a system you can map, delivery challenges, stakeholders with competing needs? If you can solve it in an afternoon, it's too small. The later assignments will feel forced.

**4. Bounded enough** — Can you actually make progress on it over the course of a self-paced program? "Redesign how my team plans work" is a spine project. "Fix my industry's talent pipeline" is not.

**5. Real constraints** — Does it have constraints that will push back on your design? Time, budget, technical limits, stakeholder needs? Unconstrained projects don't teach design — design is what happens when you can't do everything.

**6. AI tooling involved** *(preferred, not required)* — Is there AI tooling in the picture, as part of the problem or part of your solution? If yes, the course's central thesis — that framing and design matter more when implementation is cheap — will be lived experience rather than abstract argument.

## Phase 3: Commit

*Narrow to 2–3 candidates. Write a paragraph on each: what the problem is, why it fits the criteria, and what specifically is uncertain or non-obvious about it. Then pick one and commit.*

Note: you will reframe this problem in Unit 2, and your understanding of what the real problem actually is may shift more than you expect by Unit 14. The goal right now is not to pick perfectly — it's to pick something real and commit to it. A slightly imperfect choice you're actually investigating beats the ideal choice you're still deliberating about.
